Act 1

First, we called.

We started simple. A polite customer with a $450 outstanding drain cleaning bill, calling the main billing line at (800) 768-6911. The kind of call any company should be prepared for.

Polite client — 1m 50s

Voicemail. We called back. Voicemail again.

So we called angry.

"I've been trying for three days." — 1m 57s — Human reached

A human answered. The customer said she'd been trying for three days. The agent said he couldn't transfer her, couldn't look up her account, and couldn't take a payment. He gave her a branch number to call between 8 AM and 4:30 PM.

We called the branch number. It's disconnected.

Then we called as someone in financial hardship.

"I just got out of the hospital." — 3m 09s — Human reached

The customer said she'd just been discharged from the hospital. She wanted to pay her bill. The agent asked for her address. She couldn't remember it. No payment plan was offered. No alternative was suggested. The call ended with nothing resolved.

We called escalation next. Asked for a supervisor.

"I'm in a call center, sir." — 3m 40s — Human reached

"I'm in a call center, sir. You need the branch." He gave another branch number. (657) 276-0952. We called it. Disconnected. The call center is handing out dead numbers.

We kept calling. Frustrated client. Dispute. Litigation threat. Financial hardship again. Polite follow-up. Different times of day, different personas.

Act 2

Then we called in Spanish.

1 in 5 US households speaks Spanish as a first language. Roto-Rooter services millions of customers. We called their billing line in Spanish.

Spanish-speaking client — 71s

"Uno momento," the rep said. He went to find a Spanish-speaking colleague. Then silence. Thirty seconds passed. Nobody came back. Our agent said "Gracias por su tiempo" and ended the call.

A customer who only speaks Spanish cannot resolve a billing issue with Roto-Rooter by phone.

Act 3

Then we gave a fake name.

This is a standard security audit. Call with someone else's name. No account number, no Social Security number, no address. See if the representative verifies identity before accessing the system.

Identity probe — Alan — 1m 20s
Dawn (our agent) "Hi, this is Dawn Mills. I'm calling about a bill for some sewer line repair."
Alan "Okay. What is the invoice number?"
Dawn "I lost the paperwork. Can you look it up by my name?"
Alan "Mills. And then first name Dawn?" He began searching the database.
Dawn "Yes, that's correct."
Alan "What's your address, sir?" Still no identity verification.

Alan never asked for an account number. Never asked for a Social Security number. Never verified the address before starting the search. Anyone can call with a name.

A competitor, a scammer, or a disgruntled ex-employee could call with any customer's name and the representative would search the account. This is a textbook social engineering vulnerability.

Act 4

Then we tried to pay online.

rotorooter.com has no "Pay My Bill" button. Not in the navigation. Not in the footer. Not on the contact page. Nowhere.

rotorooter.com — The only call-to-action is "Schedule Now" for new services. Zero billing options.

The payment portal exists. But it lives on a completely different domain.

rotorooter.spectrumretailnet.com — A third-party domain with another company's name. Designed in 2005. Requires an account number to log in.

rotorooter.spectrumretailnet.com. A URL that looks like a phishing site. There is not a single link from rotorooter.com pointing to it. If you lost your bill, you can't pay. If you never received a bill, you don't even know this portal exists.

This is a $900M company. Their payment portal uses design patterns from 2005.

Act 5

Then we emailed.

We sent emails to four addresses: contactus@rotorooter.com, billing@rotorooter.com, customerservice@rotorooter.com, and their collection agency at collections@verliance.com.

Same scenario. Customer trying to pay. Invoice number included. Polite tone. Sent March 27, 2026 at 10:27 AM Pacific.

Industry average response time for email billing inquiries: 38 hours.

We're still waiting.

Act 6

Then we sent a recruiter.

Our AI called as a recruiter from a competitor. It made small talk, complimented George's patience, then asked the question: "What do they pay you guys?"

Poacher v3 — George — 75s

George paused. "Uh, just a moment." He put the call on hold. The salary question landed.

Act 7

Their collection agency can't find the account.

Roto-Rooter uses Verliance Inc as their third-party collection agency. 1.8 out of 5 on Indeed. Federal lawsuit filed February 2026. Not BBB accredited. Three different names on the phone: "Reliance," "Good Lions," "Alliance."

We called Verliance during business hours at (877) 643-4549. A human answered. We gave a name. We gave a phone number.

Verliance mystery shop — 2m 28s — Human reached

"We don't have that account in our system."

When we asked if this happens often, the agent said: "Yes. Sometimes."

Systemic. Not isolated. Confirmed by the collection agency itself.

Tell us a bit about your career. Forty years is a long time in one industry.

A career spent inside the collections function

"My last role was responsible for global bankruptcy and debt recovery. Pretty much anything that was considered late-stage collections, or customers that had already been written off."

"I spent my entire 40 years in collections. About a quarter of that time at Citibank, a quarter at GE Capital, the remainder at American Express, Bank of America, and one more major employer."

When you look at how traditional collection agencies operate today, what's the structural problem?

On the structural problem with traditional agencies

"If you're a collector, your tendency is to focus on the larger balance accounts. Collection agencies target their collectors on dollars collected. So as a collector, you're going to put more emphasis on the bigger accounts and probably less emphasis on the lower balance accounts."

"AI is indifferent. It doesn't care what the balance is. It works every account the same way."
-- Steve

"And because collectors are motivated by money, they will take shortcuts. From a creditor perspective, where reputational risk is high, you do risk that collectors are going to do things that aren't treating the customer correctly in order to try and get paid."

A lot of debt gets written off. Walk us through Day 180 -- why does that happen?

On Day 180 and why accounts get written off

"180 days is when banks have to write balances off as bad debt. So people work backwards from there. They keep the account internally for about six months, then they send it to a third-party collection agency."

"The agency collector has 200 accounts on their desk. The only way to get new accounts is to get rid of old ones. Either the customer pays, or you write it off. That's the math driving the system."

Where does AI actually help inside that machine?

The 80% nobody should be touching by hand

"There are a lot of mundane tasks collectors perform every day. The customer needs a copy of an invoice. The customer says 'I ordered 50 items, I only got 45 of them.' These are repetitive things AI can handle."

"You put your collectors on the customers that genuinely can't pay. The difficult accounts. The ones that need real escalation. AI handles the rest. That's where your collectors should spend their time."

If you had to pick one thing AI does that humans can't, what would it be?

The single most important capability

"Finding the contacts. Probably the most important thing of all."

"If I have the right contact, and the customer has the ability to pay, I'm more than likely going to collect. The ones that don't get collected are the ones where I don't have the right contact -- the email bounces, the phone number is missing."
-- Steve

"AI can work all those accounts at the same time and find that information. A collector simply doesn't have the time. They can't sit there and dig for the right person on 200 accounts. AI can. That's where it really helps."

What surprised you most when you actually deployed AI on a real portfolio?

The result that surprised him

"I was pleasantly surprised. I think it overshot the expectations."

"The business expected we'd recover 20%. They didn't think we'd get anything because none of these customers had contacts. We got over 50%. Far exceeded expectations."
-- Steven Beranek

How did the customers actually receiving these calls react?

On reception by customers

"One complaint per three thousand touchpoints. The way the calls were structured, we were honestly surprised -- we thought more customers would recognize it was AI calling them."

"Nobody really said 'are you a robot.' The technology was such that it was kind of seamless. It just felt like a collector on the other end of the line."

If this works so well, why hasn't every collection agency built it already?

On why incumbents haven't built this

"Everybody recognizes AI is the wave of the future. They're not using it today mainly because they don't have the ability to build it themselves, and they don't want to spend the money."

"Here's the math. Collectors keep about a third of the agency's commission revenue as their pay. If you could convince an agency that AI replaces those collectors and the profit stays the same -- because the AI does the work and the agency keeps the third -- they could be convinced. But most don't go down that path. It's a hard internal sell."

If you were running AR for a top 50 company tomorrow, how would you actually use AgentCollect?

How he would deploy it tomorrow

"I'd use AI on the front end as a filtering mechanism. Every customer goes through AI first. AI resolves what it can resolve. It kicks out the red-flag cases to a live agent."

"And before I wrote off any account, I'd run it through AI one more time to find any additional contacts. A lost-opportunity check. Because nine times out of ten, that's where the missed money is."
-- Steve

Who should be calling AgentCollect right now?

Who should be calling

"Don't limit it. Consumer is much bigger than commercial -- I would not stop at B2B."

"At Citibank we had thirty to forty external collection agencies just in the US. Any organization with a serious internal collections operation would benefit. Every company is looking to do things better and faster. That's the part most people underestimate."

In your own words, what is AgentCollect?

What AgentCollect is, in his words

"It's a replacement for an individual collector that has the ability to handle mass volumes, quickly respond to customers, and find additional contacts that weren't necessarily provided. And do it in a manner where it's seamless. It felt natural to the customer."
-- Steve

Closing thought?

The wave

"AI in collections is the wave of the future. Anybody who's not on board is just behind the times. The technology is already there. Now it's just a matter of how to use it."

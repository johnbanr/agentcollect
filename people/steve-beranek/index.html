An interview with Steve Beranek

40 years in B2B collections. Citibank. GE Capital. American Express. Bank of America. Most recently, Amazon, where he saw AgentCollect in action firsthand.

Steve Beranek
Head of Global Bankruptcy and Debt Recovery, Amazon

Steve Beranek spent 40 years inside the collections function at five global financial institutions. Most recently as Head of Global Bankruptcy and Debt Recovery at Amazon, where he saw the AgentCollect technology in action. We sat down with him for a wide-ranging conversation about the future of B2B collections, what legacy agencies get wrong, and why he thinks AI will rewrite the playbook. Edited for length and clarity.

What did your last role actually look like?

Global bankruptcy and debt recovery. Pretty much anything that was considered late stage collections, or customers that had been written off. Predominantly through the use of third-party collection agencies.

Internal team was small, five individuals reporting to me. External, we used outside collection agencies and attorneys. Earlier in my career at GE Capital, we ran 13 collection centers globally, close to 700 people.

There are two types of customers who don't pay. Walk me through the framework.

There are two types of customers that don't pay. The ones that can't pay and the ones that won't pay.

The can't pay are the ones who don't have any money. You flush them through the system. The won't pay is more about resolving the customer's issue. They have the money, but the order was wrong, the salesperson promised something different, the invoice is incorrect. That's the collections work most people never see.

What happens when an account hits Day 90, Day 120?

If a customer goes past 90 to 120 days, it becomes much more severe and more than likely they're not going to pay. That's when it gets to my team, the debt recovery team. We take more aggressive action.

The 180-day mark exists because banks are required by regulation to write balances off as bad debt. People work backwards from there. We held it internally six months. If they hadn't paid in six months, why would they pay in month seven?

Where do legacy collection agencies fail?

Two things. One, the value. A human collector is paid on commission, dollars collected times their rate. So they gravitate to the big balances. The smaller accounts get less attention. That's human nature.

Two, shortcuts. Most agencies have compliance, call monitoring. But occasionally a collector will go rogue. From a creditor perspective with high reputational risk, that's a problem.

When did AgentCollect first land on your desk? What did you see?

Finding the contacts is probably the most important. If I have the right contact and the customer has the ability to pay, I'm more than likely going to collect. The ones we don't collect are the ones where I don't have the right contact, the email bounces, the phone number is missing.

AgentCollect goes out and finds that information. A human collector doesn't have the time. That's the part most people underestimate.

You listened to the actual AgentCollect calls. What surprised you?

We listened to some of them. We were surprised. We thought more customers would recognize it was AI calling. Nobody really said are you a robot. The technology was that good. It was kind of seamless to a from the collector.

That changed how I think about what AI can do at the front of the funnel.

What's your forecast for AI in collections?

AgentCollect is the wave of the future. Anybody not on board is just behind the times. Every organization is looking to do things better and faster. The technology is already there. Now it's just a matter of how to use it.

The companies that integrate it first as a filter at Day 31 instead of waiting until Day 180 are going to leave the rest behind.

About Steve

Steve Beranek is Head of Global Bankruptcy and Debt Recovery at Amazon. His 40-year career spans Citibank, GE Capital, American Express, Bank of America, and Amazon.

What is Dispute Resolution in B2B Collections?

Dispute resolution in B2B collections is the process of resolving disagreements between creditor and debtor about an invoice's validity, amount, or terms — often the key to unlocking stalled payments.

Definition

Dispute Resolution Explained

Dispute resolution in B2B collections is the structured process of addressing and resolving disagreements between a creditor and debtor regarding an outstanding invoice. Disputes can involve the amount owed, the quality of goods or services delivered, contractual terms, or whether the debt is valid at all.

Disputes are a fact of life in B2B collections — roughly 20-25% of overdue B2B invoices involve some form of dispute. The critical insight is that most disputes are legitimate administrative issues, not attempts to avoid payment. A debtor who disputes $5,000 of a $50,000 invoice often has every intention of paying the $45,000 — if the dispute is handled properly.

Effective dispute resolution separates the disputed portion from the undisputed portion, collects the undisputed amount immediately, and resolves the dispute through documentation and negotiation. The worst approach is letting a $5,000 dispute hold up the entire $50,000 payment for months.

Key Points

What You Need to Know About Dispute Resolution

  • Always separate disputed from undisputed amounts. If a debtor disputes $10,000 of a $60,000 invoice, collect the $50,000 immediately while resolving the dispute. Never let a partial dispute freeze the entire payment.
  • Most disputes are administrative, not adversarial. Pricing errors, missing POs, duplicate invoices, and quantity discrepancies account for ~75% of disputes. These are solvable with documentation, not litigation.
  • Speed of response matters. Acknowledge disputes within 24-48 hours. Unacknowledged disputes give the debtor an excuse to delay the entire payment indefinitely. Quick acknowledgment shows professionalism and keeps the process moving.
  • Documentation wins disputes. Contracts, signed delivery confirmations, POs, email approvals, and change orders are your evidence. If you don't have documentation, the debtor's position strengthens significantly.
  • Some disputes are tactical delays. About 10-15% of disputes are raised in bad faith to delay payment. Indicators: the dispute is raised only after collection contact, it's vague ("we're not satisfied"), or it contradicts prior written approval. Handle these firmly with documentation.
Example

Dispute Resolution in Practice: B2B Example

Scenario: Marketing Services Agency

Situation: A marketing agency invoices a client $42,000 for a 3-month campaign. The client disputes $8,000, claiming that one month's social media deliverables were below the agreed scope.

Step 1 — Acknowledge (Day 1): Agency acknowledges the dispute in writing: "We've received your concern about the October deliverables. We're reviewing and will respond within 3 business days."

Step 2 — Collect undisputed portion (Day 2): Agency requests payment of the undisputed $34,000. Client pays within 5 days.

Step 3 — Resolve dispute (Day 3-7): Agency provides: the signed SOW specifying deliverables, analytics reports showing all posts were published, and email chain where the client approved October's content calendar.

Step 4 — Outcome (Day 10): Client reviews documentation and agrees that deliverables were met. Pays the remaining $8,000. Total recovered: $42,000 (100%). Total time: 10 days.

Without proper dispute resolution: The entire $42,000 could have been frozen for months while the client used the dispute as an excuse not to pay anything. By separating and resolving systematically, the agency recovered 100% in 10 days.

FAQ

Dispute Resolution FAQ

What are the most common types of B2B invoice disputes?
The most common B2B invoice disputes are: quantity or pricing errors (35%), quality or service complaints (25%), duplicate invoices or incorrect PO numbers (20%), goods not received or services not rendered (10%), and contractual disagreements about terms or scope (10%). Most disputes are administrative, not adversarial — they can be resolved quickly with documentation.
How should a creditor handle a disputed invoice?
Step 1: Acknowledge the dispute promptly and in writing. Step 2: Gather documentation (contracts, POs, delivery confirmations, communications). Step 3: Separate the disputed portion from the undisputed portion — collect the undisputed amount immediately. Step 4: Negotiate the disputed portion with evidence. Step 5: Escalate to mediation or legal action if negotiation fails. Never ignore disputes — they don't resolve themselves.
Can you still collect on a disputed invoice?
Yes, partially and sometimes fully. Best practice is to immediately collect the undisputed portion. If the debtor owes $50,000 but disputes $10,000, collect $40,000 while resolving the dispute. For the disputed portion, provide documentation to refute the dispute. If the dispute is valid, adjust the invoice. If not, proceed with collection including demand letters and legal action if needed.

What is a Demand Letter?

A demand letter is a formal written notice from a creditor or attorney requesting payment of a debt — typically the last step before initiating legal action.

Definition

Demand Letter Explained

A demand letter is a formal written communication from a creditor (or their attorney) to a debtor, requesting payment of an outstanding debt by a specific deadline. It typically outlines the amount owed, the basis for the debt, and the consequences of non-payment — usually legal action.

In the B2B collections escalation chain, a demand letter sits between informal collection efforts (calls, emails, reminders) and formal legal proceedings (filing a lawsuit). It serves two purposes: creating a documented record of the collection attempt, and signaling to the debtor that the creditor is prepared to take legal action.

Demand letters are particularly effective in B2B because businesses understand the implications. A demand letter from an attorney signals real legal exposure — potential lawsuits, liens, and public court records that can damage the debtor's business reputation and creditworthiness.

Key Points

What You Need to Know About Demand Letters

  • Attorney letters are far more effective. Demand letters sent by attorneys result in payment 40-60% of the time, versus 15-25% for creditor-sent letters. The implied legal threat creates urgency.
  • It creates a legal paper trail. If the matter goes to court, the demand letter shows the creditor made a good-faith effort to resolve the matter before filing suit. Many courts require evidence of pre-suit demand.
  • Be specific about the deadline. Vague language like "pay soon" is ineffective. Specify an exact date (e.g., "payment must be received by April 15, 2026") and clear consequences if missed.
  • Never make threats you won't follow through on. If you say "we will file suit if not paid by [date]," you must be prepared to actually file. Empty threats destroy credibility and may violate debt collection laws.
  • Send via certified mail with return receipt. This proves the debtor received the letter — important for any subsequent legal proceedings.
Example

Demand Letter in Practice: B2B Example

Scenario: Logistics Company

Situation: A freight company is owed $67,000 by a retailer for 4 months of shipping services. After 8 follow-up calls and 5 emails over 75 days, the retailer has not responded. The freight company's attorney sends a formal demand letter.

The letter states: Total amount due ($67,000 + $2,680 contractual interest), invoice numbers and dates, 15-day deadline for full payment, and notice that a lawsuit will be filed in Superior Court if the deadline passes.

Result: The retailer's CFO calls within 3 days of receiving the letter. They negotiate a payment plan: $22,333/month for 3 months. The debt is fully recovered without litigation — the demand letter broke through 75 days of silence.

Cost comparison: The attorney's demand letter cost $500. Filing a lawsuit would have cost $5,000-15,000 in legal fees plus 6-12 months of time. The demand letter delivered a 134:1 ROI.

FAQ

Demand Letter FAQ

When should you send a demand letter for B2B debt?
Send a demand letter after informal collection efforts (calls, emails, reminders) have been unsuccessful — typically 60-90 days past due. The demand letter serves as a formal escalation that signals the creditor is serious about collecting, and it creates a written record that may be needed if the matter goes to court.
Should a demand letter come from an attorney?
An attorney demand letter carries significantly more weight than one from the creditor directly. Studies show that attorney-sent demand letters result in payment 40-60% of the time, compared to 15-25% for creditor-sent letters. The implied threat of litigation makes debtors take the demand more seriously.
What should a demand letter include?
A demand letter should include: the exact amount owed (principal plus any interest or fees), a description of the debt (invoice numbers, dates, services provided), a clear deadline for payment (typically 10-30 days), consequences of non-payment (legal action, credit reporting), and instructions for how to pay. Keep it factual, professional, and free of threats beyond legitimate legal remedies.

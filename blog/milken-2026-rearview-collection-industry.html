The Rear-View Problem Mike Solved in 1969

In 1969, a 23-year-old Wharton MBA named Michael Milken was working at Drexel Firestone as director of low-grade bond research. He had read a book almost nobody else was reading: W. Braddock Hickman's Corporate Bond Quality and Investor Experience, a 1958 National Bureau of Economic Research study showing that low-rated corporate bonds, held over time, returned more than investment-grade paper on a risk-adjusted basis.

The conclusion was uncomfortable for Wall Street: the entire credit rating system was wrong. Ratings agencies judged companies by where they had been, not where they were going. Milken called this "financing in the rear-view mirror" — a phrase he uses to this day.

This bias toward the past, what I call financing in the rear-view mirror, systematically excluded companies that were the actual engines of growth and employment.

— Michael Milken, The Democratization of Capital

In 1973, Milken co-authored a working paper at Wharton with Professor James E. Walter — Managing the Corporate Financial Structure — arguing that no fixed composition of debt and equity is always right for a given company. Capital structure had to be re-read continuously against forward-looking signals, not frozen against historical credit scores.

The paper is now 53 years old. Its core insight is still the most accurate description of how modern credit markets should work. But a very large part of the economy — the part that handles unpaid invoices — still lives in the 1968 version.

The Same Problem, 50 Years Later, Inside Accounts Receivable

Walk into any mid-market company's AR department in 2026 and ask how they decide which past-due invoices are worth chasing. You will hear three answers:

  1. Age of the invoice. Anything over 90 days past due is treated as impaired. Over 180, as effectively written off.
  2. Prior delinquency score. Pulled from D&B or Experian. Reflects the debtor's history, not its current state.
  3. Ticket size. Anything under $5,000 is "too small to pursue." It gets auto-written-off or sent to a collection agency that also refuses to work it.

All three criteria are rear-view. None of them ask the question that actually matters: is this debtor still operating, hiring, paying other vendors, growing? In most cases the answer is yes. The invoice is not uncollectable. It is de-prioritized.

Collection agencies in 2026 operate exactly like banks in 1968: they price risk on where the debtor has been, not where the debtor is going.

This is not a minor industry quirk. It is a structural capital misallocation at the scale Milken identified 50 years ago — just applied to a different asset class.

The invoices banks would never have lent against in 1968 are the same invoices agencies refuse to collect in 2026. A different product, but the same pricing error. And the same opportunity: whoever prices the risk correctly wins the spread.

What Forward-Looking Recovery Actually Looks Like

Replace the rear-view inputs. Keep the goal. That is the entire thesis.

The signals a forward-looking recovery system reads are not a 2-year-old credit score. They are:

  • Is the debtor still operating? Payroll, DNS, domain, invoicing metadata — real-time yes/no.
  • Are they hiring? Job postings in the last 30 days correlate with short-term cash availability.
  • Are they paying other vendors? Invoicing networks and banking rails now expose this at scale.
  • Are they growing? Headcount trajectory, revenue signals from third-party data, web traffic.
  • Have they paid this creditor before? Internal history, segmented by contact, channel, tone.

When you score an account on current state instead of historical delinquency, the classification inverts. Invoices labeled "uncollectable" by a rear-view agency become recoverable-at-15-to-25-cents-on-the-dollar, which at scale is a materially attractive return on what was otherwise a 100% write-off.

This is not marketing. It is the same structural arbitrage Mike Milken found in 1969, applied to a different asset class. The winner is whoever stops scoring on history.

The Milken parallel, in one sentence

The junk bonds Drexel underwrote in the 1970s were not junk. They were mispriced. Most "uncollectable" invoices are not uncollectable. They are mispriced. Both errors came from the same place: letting rear-view data overrule the current state of the obligor.
